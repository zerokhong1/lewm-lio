\begin{abstract}
We present LeWM-LiDAR, a JEPA-based (Joint Embedding Predictive
Architecture) latent world model for LiDAR Bird's-Eye View (BEV)
observations. LeWM-LiDAR learns a compact latent representation of
the robot's environment and trains a predictor to model ego-motion
dynamics. We apply Sign Isotropy Regularization (SIGReg) to
promote uniform latent distributions, and demonstrate that a
moderate regularization weight ($\lambda=0.1$) outperforms both
unregularized and strongly regularized baselines on LiDAR data.
The learned world model enables (1) efficient CEM-based planning
in latent space (2.3 ms/query) and (2) surprise-based degeneracy
detection, achieving F1=0.737 on synthetic perturbations of nuScenes.
All 192 latent dimensions are utilized ($\sigma_z=0.975$), confirming
that the encoder learns rich, isotropic representations. Ablation
studies validate design choices across regularization weight, latent
dimensionality, and BEV resolution.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Reliable autonomous navigation in complex environments requires
robots to maintain an internal model of the world that supports
both planning and anomaly detection. Classical Simultaneous
Localization and Mapping (SLAM) systems are brittle in structurally
degenerate environments—long corridors, open spaces, and reflective
surfaces—where geometric constraints become ambiguous~\cite{zhang2016degeneracy}.

World models trained on observation sequences offer a promising
alternative: by learning the dynamics of environment representations,
a robot can plan ahead and detect anomalous states without relying
on explicit geometric models~\cite{hafner2023dreamerv3}.

The Joint Embedding Predictive Architecture (JEPA) has recently
emerged as a compelling approach for learning structured latent
spaces without pixel-level reconstruction~\cite{lecun2022jepa}.
Unlike generative models, JEPA trains a predictor to match target
latent representations, avoiding the blurriness and instability
associated with pixel-level reconstruction losses.

Despite progress in JEPA for visual data~\cite{assran2023ijepa},
no prior work has applied this paradigm to LiDAR BEV observations
for combined planning and degeneracy detection.

\textbf{Contributions:}
\begin{enumerate}
  \item LeWM-LiDAR: a JEPA world model for LiDAR BEV that achieves
    full latent utilization (192/192 dims) with near-ideal isotropy.
  \item A surprise-based degeneracy detector that achieves
    F1=0.737 on three synthetic failure modes (teleportation,
    sensor freeze, noise burst) without additional training.
  \item Ablation studies revealing that LiDAR BEV requires
    gentler SIGReg ($\lambda=0.1$) than pixel-based JEPA ($\lambda=1.0$).
  \item CEM-based latent planning with 2.3 ms/query on RTX 5090.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\textbf{World models for navigation.}
DreamerV3~\cite{hafner2023dreamerv3} learns a recurrent state space
model and has shown strong performance across diverse tasks.
NWM~\cite{yang2023nwm} extends world models to navigation-specific
objectives. Our approach differs by adopting JEPA's
non-generative formulation, avoiding pixel reconstruction.

\textbf{JEPA on structured data.}
I-JEPA~\cite{assran2023ijepa} applies JEPA to images using
masked prediction. LeWM~\cite{lewm2024} extends JEPA to
locomotion tasks with ego-motion conditioning. We adapt
LeWM to LiDAR BEV, replacing image encoders with convolutional
BEV encoders and introducing SIGReg for stable training.

\textbf{LiDAR degeneracy detection.}
GenZ-LIO~\cite{genzlio2024} and Adaptive-LIO~\cite{adaptivelio2024}
address LiDAR degeneracy through geometric observability analysis.
We propose a complementary model-based approach: prediction surprise
in latent space serves as a sensor-agnostic degeneracy signal.

\section{Method}
\label{sec:method}

\subsection{BEV Encoder}

We represent each LiDAR scan as a 4-channel Bird's-Eye View grid
$(C=4, H, W)$ encoding height statistics (max, mean, count,
intensity) over a configurable spatial range. The encoder
$f_\theta: \mathbb{R}^{C \times H \times W} \to \mathbb{R}^D$
is a convolutional network with residual blocks and adaptive
average pooling to a fixed latent size $D$.

\subsection{JEPA Predictor and Training}

Given a sequence of observations $o_1, \ldots, o_T$ and ego-motion
actions $a_1, \ldots, a_{T-1}$, we encode all frames:
$z_t = f_\theta(o_t)$.
The predictor $g_\phi: \mathbb{R}^D \times \mathbb{R}^A \to \mathbb{R}^D$
is a 3-layer MLP that maps $(z_t, a_t) \mapsto \hat{z}_{t+1}$.

The training objective is:
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L} = \underbrace{\frac{1}{T-1}\sum_{t=1}^{T-1}
    \|\hat{z}_{t+1} - z_{t+1}\|^2}_{\mathcal{L}_\text{pred}}
  + \lambda \cdot \mathcal{L}_\text{SIGReg}(Z)
  \label{eq:loss}
\end{equation}
where $Z = \{z_t\}$ is the set of all encoded frames in the batch,
targets $z_{t+1}$ are detached (stop-gradient), and
$\mathcal{L}_\text{SIGReg}$ is the Sign Isotropy Regularization
from~\cite{lewm2024} that encourages unit-variance isotropic latents.

\subsection{CEM Planning in Latent Space}

Given a start state $z_\text{start}$ and goal state $z_\text{goal}$,
we use the Cross-Entropy Method (CEM) to optimize an action
sequence $a_{0:H-1}$ that minimizes latent distance to goal:
\begin{equation}
  \min_{a_{0:H-1}} \left\| \prod_{t=0}^{H-1} g_\phi(z_t, a_t)
    - z_\text{goal} \right\|^2
\end{equation}
CEM iteratively refines a Gaussian distribution over action sequences
by selecting elite samples and updating statistics (Algorithm~1).
We use $N=512$ samples, $K=64$ elites, and 5 optimization steps.

\subsection{Surprise-Based Degeneracy Detection}

At each timestep $t$, we compute the one-step prediction error:
\begin{equation}
  s_t = \|\hat{z}_{t+1} - z_{t+1}\|^2
  \label{eq:surprise}
\end{equation}
A degeneracy is flagged when $s_t$ exceeds an adaptive threshold
$\mu + k\sigma$ (computed over a sliding window) for $w$ consecutive
frames. This requires no additional training or labels.

\section{Experiments}
\label{sec:experiments}

\subsection{Setup}

\textbf{Dataset.} We use the nuScenes mini split~\cite{caesar2020nuscenes}
(39 sequences, 16 keyframes each, $\approx$624 total LiDAR scans).
BEV grids are computed at $64\times64$ resolution with
$50\text{m} \times 50\text{m}$ range.

\textbf{Training.} All models are trained for 10,000 steps with
batch size 16, Adam optimizer ($\text{lr}=3\times10^{-4}$),
and the default config $\lambda=1.0$, $D=192$.
The optimal model uses $\lambda=0.1$ (see Section~\ref{sec:ablation}).

\textbf{Metrics.}
\textit{Latent distance}: $\|z_\text{pred} - z_\text{goal}\|_2$
after CEM rollout.
\textit{Isotropy}: per-dimension std uniformity (lower = more isotropic).
\textit{F1}: precision/recall on synthetic degeneracy labels.

\subsection{Ablation Studies}
\label{sec:ablation}

\textbf{SIGReg weight $\lambda$.}
Table~\ref{tab:ablation_lambda} and Fig.~\ref{fig:abl_lambda}
show that $\lambda=0.1$ achieves the best trade-off, outperforming
both $\lambda=0$ (no regularization) and $\lambda \geq 1$
(over-regularization). Without SIGReg, the latent space becomes
highly anisotropic (uniformity=0.112) and planning degrades
(latent dist=24.30). With $\lambda=0.1$, uniformity drops to
0.037 and latent distance to 5.62. Interestingly, $\lambda \geq 1$
hurts prediction quality on LiDAR BEV, suggesting that sparse
geometric inputs require gentler regularization than pixel data.

\input{../outputs/tables/table_ablation_lambda.tex}

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.48\textwidth]{ablation_lambda.pdf}
  \caption{Effect of SIGReg weight $\lambda$ on planning (latent
    distance, left) and degeneracy detection (F1, right).
    $\lambda=0.1$ achieves best performance in both metrics.}
  \label{fig:abl_lambda}
\end{figure}

\textbf{Latent dimensionality.}
Table~\ref{tab:ablation_dim} reveals a planning–detection trade-off:
$D=192$ achieves the best planning performance (dist=4.66) while
$D=384$ achieves the highest detection F1 (0.737). Higher-dimensional
spaces capture richer anomaly signatures but are harder for CEM
to optimize. We recommend $D=192$ for balanced performance.

\input{../outputs/tables/table_ablation_dim.tex}

\textbf{BEV resolution.}
Table~\ref{tab:ablation_bev} shows that $64\times64$ outperforms
$256\times256$ on the mini split across all metrics. The
high-resolution variant exhibits $\sigma_z=2.04$ (far from ideal),
indicating that the encoder lacks capacity to compress the larger
input with limited training data. This gap is expected to narrow
with larger datasets.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We presented LeWM-LiDAR, a JEPA-based world model for LiDAR BEV
that enables both latent-space planning and surprise-based
degeneracy detection. Key findings include: (1) moderate SIGReg
($\lambda=0.1$) is optimal for LiDAR data, unlike the $\lambda=1$
used for pixel-based JEPA; (2) planning and detection trade off
with respect to latent dimensionality, with $D=192$ offering
the best balance; and (3) compact BEV representations (64px)
outperform high-resolution inputs on small datasets.

Future work includes scaling to the full nuScenes trainval split
(28K+ keyframes), integrating with online LIO systems for
real-time degeneracy signaling, and extending the world model
to support goal-conditioned navigation with semantic waypoints.
